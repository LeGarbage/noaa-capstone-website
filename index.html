NOAA WATER COLUMN SONAR VISUALIZATION

For Warren Tech 2025-2026 Capstone

THE PROBLEM

Water column sonar is an acoustic backscatter from the water’s surface to the seafloor which is used to assess physical and biological characteristics of the ocean. With this data scientists determine the spatio-temporal distributions of different marine life including many species of fish.


Currently scientists use expensive and proprietary software to visualize the water column. Open source efforts such as echofish (shown above) are being developed to allow users access to these datasets but those efforts fall short, only enabling small glimpses of the data spanning a couple hours at most.


The goal of this project is to take these large cruises observing months of data at a time and allow users to more seamlessly visualize them at scale. The intuition that someone has when I hand them a phone with a picture on it is that they can hold the phone and zoom in and out to get a better understanding of the content — water column sonar data needs to break free from the constraint of its size so users can follow that same intuition. Using modern tools in the browser will allow users to quickly download small subsets of the data while still having access to that comprehensive view.

OUR STATEMENT AND GOALS

Environmental impacts and sustainability are the central focus of this project. Being able to analyze and visualize the data at scale gives researchers the tools to understand patterns and develop models. Understanding characteristics of the marine biome enables scientists to more accurately estimate fish population and characterize behavior, this has a direct impact preventing the collapse of keystone species who have historically been prone to overfishing throughout the world's oceans. The fisheries scientists who look at this data ultimately use it to determine things like which species are added to the Marine Mammal Protections Act or the Endangered Species Act, which in turn can protect marine wildlife in critical habitats.


We can also start to model this data alongside meteorological observations which reveals confounding effects associated with climate change. Being able to access decades of data helps us understand that environmental impact over time.


And finally, consolidated visualizations of cruise data creates a canvas where deep learning models can begin to inform users which regions are of the most interest. Working with researchers at CU Boulder, we are beginning to add semantic layers to these datasets and enrich the visualization process but the derived data suffers from the same visualization challenges — the data is too big to look at. We need ways to more intuitively navigate this process to unleash its full utility.
